The Situation

An intracity delivery network at scale

18,000+ daily orders across multiple metros. Drivers are independent contractors who choose when and where to work. Demand is highly spatio-temporal — concentrated in specific zones during predictable windows. The platform uses surge pricing as the primary supply-demand balancing mechanism.

The Reframe

This isn't a supply problem. It's a timing problem.

The default assumption: "failed deliveries happen because there aren't enough drivers." But total active drivers during peak hours is sufficient — the issue is that surge pricing only activates after orders are already queuing. By the time drivers 3km away respond, the demand window has passed.

The real problem: the system can only react to demand that already exists. It can't act on demand that's about to exist.

Surge pricing solves scarcity by making it expensive. But if scarcity is caused by lag, not shortage, the answer isn't price — it's prediction.

What can't change

Why Option C: Individual-zone optimization always creates neighboring problems. The only way to avoid supply cannibalization is to treat the entire city's idle driver pool as a single optimization problem — not N independent problems.

The Bet

Predictive geofencing with global supply optimization

The bet is that you can predict where demand will be in 25 minutes with enough confidence to pre-position drivers — and that incentive-based nudges will get enough acceptance to meaningfully shift local supply.

Three design choices that define this bet:

  • H3 hexagonal grid (res 8, ~460m): Small enough for actionable positioning, large enough to aggregate meaningful signal. Replaces rigid neighborhood polygons.
  • Global assignment, not greedy individual nudges: Every 90 seconds, treat all idle drivers as resources and all demand centroids as soft targets. Minimize total system travel while respecting zone balance constraints.
  • Incentive-based, never mandatory: Drivers see a "Demand Preview" card with expected earnings. They choose. Mandatory routing was tested and killed driver satisfaction. Trading ~5% positioning efficiency for retention is the right call at scale.

Key Tradeoffs

The hard calls

  • Incentive nudges vs mandatory routing: Mandatory routing gets ~95% positioning accuracy but damages driver trust and increases churn. Incentive-based nudges get ~73% acceptance — close enough to optimal without the retention cost. At 18K orders/day, driver retention matters more than marginal positioning.
  • Global optimization vs per-zone optimization: Global is significantly more complex to build and debug. But per-zone optimization provably causes oscillation — each zone's optimizer is unaware of its effect on neighbors. This isn't a "nice to have" architectural choice, it's a correctness requirement.
  • 25-minute forecast horizon vs longer: Longer horizons (45 min) have lower accuracy. Shorter horizons (10 min) don't give drivers enough time to reposition. 25 min is the sweet spot where prediction confidence is still >70% and travel time from adjacent zones is feasible.
Open Questions

What could go wrong

Forecast accuracy in novel events: The model trains on historical patterns. What happens during a cricket match, a sudden downpour, or a new restaurant launch? The system has no prior for these. A fallback to reactive surge for "low-confidence" predictions might be necessary.

Driver gaming: If drivers learn that positioning in certain zones earns better nudge bonuses, they might cluster there preemptively — creating artificial supply gluts. The optimizer needs to account for organic driver movement, not just nudge-driven movement.

Fairness across driver cohorts: Drivers who accept nudges earn more. Drivers who don't accept (perhaps because they can't move easily) earn less. Does this create a two-tier driver economy? Need to monitor earnings distribution, not just average.
