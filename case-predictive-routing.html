01 / The Problem

Surge Pricing Is Fundamentally Too Late

In intracity logistics, surge pricing is reactive by design. By the time a demand spike is detected — enough orders queuing without driver accepts — and a 1.5x multiplier is applied, the local supply of drivers is already depleted. Drivers sitting 3km away need 15-20 minutes to reach the surge zone.

By then, the demand spike has often passed. Customers have churned. Drivers who moved got no trips. The surge mechanism — designed to solve scarcity — created a lag-induced scarcity-frustration loop.

At 18,000+ daily orders, this was responsible for a 22% zero-dispatch rate during peak evening hours. That's 4,000 failed deliveries a day not because we lacked drivers — but because they were in the wrong place at the wrong time.

"The problem wasn't supply. It was spatial mismatch. We had drivers — we just couldn't see where demand was going before it arrived."

02 / The Hacky V1

Manual Dispatch Hints via Push Notifications

Before building the ML pipeline, we tested the hypothesis manually. Operations leads would review historical data on Monday mornings, identify predictable high-demand events for the week (tech park dispersals, evening grocery windows, major residential towers), and schedule manual push notifications to idle nearby drivers 30 minutes prior.

The message was blunt: "High demand expected in Koramangala. Move there to earn more."

This worked. A 5% increase in local supply before the surge started was measurable and statistically significant. The hypothesis was validated: if drivers know demand is coming, they will move.

But manual scheduling couldn't scale beyond 3 cities or adapt in real time to unexpected spikes.

03 / The Wall

Supply Cannibalization

When we automated the pre-emptive dispatch nudges using a simple demand forecast model, we hit an immediate unintended consequence: Supply Cannibalization.

By aggressively drawing drivers toward Zone A (predicted high demand), we drained adjacent Zone B. Zone B would then trigger its own surge. Drivers would move from Zone A to Zone B. Zone A would surge again. The supply oscillated between zones like a pendulum.

We needed a model that didn't maximize supply in a single zone — it had to optimize the global distribution of supply across all overlapping geofences simultaneously, respecting driver trajectory and minimum earn guarantees.

04 / The Real Solution

Predictive Geofencing with Global Supply Optimization

We rebuilt the dispatch system around three core architectural changes:

  • H3 Hexagonal Grid (Resolution 8): Instead of rigid neighborhood polygons, we shifted to Uber's H3 library to generate dynamic, overlapping hexagonal grids. Each hex is ~460m across — small enough to be actionable, large enough to aggregate meaningful signal.
  • Multi-Signal Demand Forecast: The demand model ingested historical order density (by hour, day, and location), real-time traffic anomalies from the Maps API, and weather signals. A gradient-boosted tree predicted demand 25 minutes forward for each H3 hex.
  • Global Supply Optimization: Instead of nudging individual drivers, the system ran a global assignment problem every 90 seconds — treating idle drivers as resources and predicted demand centroids as "soft assignments," minimizing total system travel time while respecting driver constraints.

Critically, the nudge to drivers was incentive-based, not mandatory. Drivers heading toward a predicted high-demand zone received a "Demand Preview" card in the app: "High orders likely in X area in 20 mins. Route there to maximize earnings." Accept rate of the nudge was tracked as a secondary metric.

05 / Results & Learnings

The Impact Compounded

PhaseChangeOutcome
V1 (Manual) Manual push notifications for 3 predictable demand patterns +5% local supply pre-spike. Hypothesis validated.
V2 (Naive Auto) Simple forecast model → single-zone optimization Supply cannibalization. NPS dropped 8 pts in affected cities.
V3 (Final) H3 grid + global optimizer + incentive nudge −18% zero-dispatch rate. P80 pickup 24→16 min. −12% empty legs.

The most important learning: supply optimization at the individual-driver level always produces global inefficiency. The only correct level of abstraction is the full supply graph — treating every idle driver simultaneously, not greedily reassigning each one.

We also learned that driver autonomy matters. Mandatory routing reduced driver satisfaction and increased churn. Incentive-based nudges with full transparency (showing the driver why and what they'd earn) had a 73% acceptance rate with no meaningful CSAT impact.
