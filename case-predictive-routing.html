Context

The Setting

An intracity delivery network processing 18,000+ daily orders across multiple metropolitan zones. Drivers are independent contractors who choose when and where to work. Demand is highly spatio-temporal — concentrated in specific zones during predictable windows (lunch, evening grocery, office dispersal).

The platform uses surge pricing as its primary mechanism to balance supply and demand in real time.

Problem

Surge Pricing Is Fundamentally Reactive

Surge pricing only activates after demand has already spiked and orders are queuing without driver accepts. By the time a 1.5x multiplier is applied, the local driver pool is already depleted. Drivers 3km away need 15-20 minutes to reach the surge zone — by which point, the demand spike has often passed.

The result: a 22% zero-dispatch rate during peak evening hours. That's ~4,000 failed deliveries per day — not because of insufficient total supply, but because drivers were consistently in the wrong place at the wrong time.

The problem wasn't supply. It was spatial mismatch. Drivers existed — but the system couldn't see where demand was going before it arrived.

Approaches Tried

What Didn't Work (and Why)

ApproachWhat It DidWhy It Failed
Manual Nudges Ops leads identified predictable demand events and pushed notifications to idle drivers 30 min before +5% local supply pre-spike. Validated the hypothesis but couldn't scale beyond 3 cities or adapt to unexpected spikes.
Naive Auto-Forecast Simple demand forecast model optimized supply for a single zone at a time Supply cannibalization — drawing drivers to Zone A drained Zone B, causing oscillating surges between adjacent zones.

The core failure of the naive approach: optimizing supply for individual zones creates global inefficiency. Solving one zone's scarcity by pulling from neighbors just moves the problem.

Solution

Predictive Geofencing with Global Supply Optimization

The solution required three architectural changes that worked together:

  • H3 Hexagonal Grid (Resolution 8): Replaced rigid neighborhood polygons with Uber's H3 library — dynamic, overlapping hexagonal cells ~460m across. Small enough for actionable precision, large enough to aggregate meaningful signal.
  • Multi-Signal Demand Forecast (T+25 min): A gradient-boosted tree model ingesting historical order density (hour/day/location), real-time traffic anomalies from Maps API, and weather signals. Predicts demand 25 minutes forward per H3 hex.
  • Global Supply Optimizer: Instead of nudging individual drivers toward individual zones, the system runs a global assignment problem every 90 seconds — treating all idle drivers as resources and all predicted demand centroids as soft assignments, minimizing total system travel time while respecting driver constraints.

Key Insight

Why This Worked

Supply optimization at the individual-driver level always produces global inefficiency. The only correct level of abstraction is the full supply graph — treating every idle driver simultaneously, not greedily reassigning each one.

The second insight: driver autonomy matters more than optimal positioning. Incentive-based nudges with full transparency (showing why and expected earnings) achieved 73% acceptance — close enough to optimal routing without the trust erosion of mandatory dispatch.
