The Story

My background is CS, but I ended up in product because the problems I find hardest aren't technical — they're the ones where you've built the right system and people still don't use it the way you expected.

At Delhivery, I own intracity: the algorithms that match drivers to orders, the surge pricing that adjusts in real time, and the acceptance mechanics that determine whether a driver takes a trip or waits for something better. It's a marketplace where changing one parameter in the matching logic affects pickup times across six cities within the hour. I've shipped things that worked, and a few that didn't — the surge pricing case study on this page is one of the latter.

Before that at Urbanladder, I built UrbanInteriors from nothing. The brief was vague: make interior design a business. I spent the first month manually matching customers to designers on WhatsApp before writing a line of product spec. That manual phase is still the best product research I've done.

My MBA thesis looked at why digital payment adoption stalled in certain segments despite good UX. The answer wasn't UX — it was incentive structure and reference price framing. I use that lens a lot: when a metric isn't moving, the first question isn't "what's broken" but "what does the user think this costs them."

What I'm Looking For
⚙️
Problems where the data doesn't point to an obvious answer
I'm not interested in roles where the PM job is writing specs for features engineering already knows how to build. The work I want is figuring out why acceptance rate dropped when nothing changed, or why conversion is lower in one segment than the pricing model predicts.
📐
Engineers who push back
The best product decisions I've made came out of an engineer telling me my requirement was wrong. I want a team where that happens regularly, not one where the PM's word is final by default.
🏗️
A product where I can be wrong visibly
Marketplaces and consumer products are good for this — the data is fast and the consequences are real. A bad pricing decision shows up in the numbers within a week. That feedback loop is what I'm looking for in the next role.
