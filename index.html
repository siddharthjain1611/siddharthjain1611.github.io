The Story

I started in computer science at IIITM Gwalior and ended up in product management — not by accident, but because I found the space between technical systems and human decisions to be the most interesting place to work.

At Delhivery, I own the intracity marketplace: the algorithms that match drivers to orders, the pricing that balances supply and demand in real time, and the earner-side experience that determines whether a driver accepts a trip. It's the kind of product where a small change in one variable has immediate, measurable consequences across six cities and tens of thousands of daily orders.

Before that, at Urbanladder (Reliance Retail), I built an interior design product line from scratch — which sounds different but involves the same core problem: connecting supply to demand efficiently, defining the right metrics, and iterating fast enough to matter.

My MBA thesis studied why people resist digital payments. I surveyed 300+ users using the KANO model and found that the usual answers — UX, trust, awareness — were less predictive than incentive design and transaction cost framing. That kind of finding from primary research is what I find most useful in product work.

I care about three things in any PM role: a clear problem that is genuinely hard, engineers and data scientists who want to debate the solution, and the ability to measure whether what I shipped actually worked.

"The most interesting PM problems are the ones where the system and the human are in tension with each other."
