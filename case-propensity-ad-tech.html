Context

The Setting

A retail media network — one of the fastest-growing ad channels — built on sponsored search ads within an e-commerce platform. Historically, ad targeting relied on two signals: third-party cookies for cross-site tracking, and purchase history data to build audience segments.

The targeting logic was intuitive: if a user bought running shoes last month, show them running gear ads today.

Problem

Two Simultaneous Structural Shifts

Two things broke this model at the same time:

  • Cookie deprecation: Chrome followed Firefox and Safari in stripping third-party cookies, eliminating cross-site tracking.
  • Signal staleness: Purchase history ages poorly. What a user bought 3 weeks ago predicts their current intent far less reliably than what they clicked on in the last 90 seconds.

Advertisers were watching CTR fall quarter-over-quarter. The ad product wasn't broken — the targeting signal was stale.

Purchase history tells you what someone wanted. Session behavior tells you what they want right now. The gap between those two is where ad dollars go to die.

The category boosting V1 was fast to ship — if a user clicked two "Electronics" items, apply a flat 1.3x bid multiplier. It proved the directional hypothesis (in-session behavior is predictive) but had fundamental flaws:

  • A 0.8-second accidental click was treated identically to a 4-minute deep-read on a product spec sheet
  • "Laptops" and "Phone Cases" were both "Electronics" — same boost, fundamentally different conversion propensities
  • No model of whether someone was browsing casually or actively comparing final options
Solution

In-Session Behavioral DNN with Velocity Features

The solution replaced categorical signals with a purpose-engineered feature set that captured the physics of user intent — how someone engages, not just whether they engaged.

Four key engineering decisions:

  • Velocity features over raw clicks: Time-normalized interaction rates — scroll depth per second, dwell time relative to content length, image-vs-text attention ratio. Captures how someone engages, not just whether they engaged.
  • Ephemeral session graph: A lightweight in-memory graph tracking interaction sequences. A user who viewed → filtered → zoomed → read specs has a fundamentally different topology than view → back → view → back.
  • DNN trained on conversion-as-truth: Sessions labeled by eventual conversion within 72 hours. A shallow DNN (3 hidden layers) predicts this from velocity features alone. The model never sees user IDs or historical data.
  • SKU-cluster targeting: Output is propensity score per item cluster (groups of 8-12 semantically similar SKUs) — much more precise than category-level boosting.

Key Insight

Why This Worked

Recency dominates history. The in-session propensity engine outperformed cookie-based targeting by 22% CTR — even when cookies had access to 30 days of purchase data. What a user is doing right now is a stronger signal than what they bought last month.

The privacy constraint, which initially felt like a limitation, became a forcing function for better product design. Stripping away identity forced a deeper understanding of session behavior than targeting tools historically required. Privacy compliance became a competitive advantage.
