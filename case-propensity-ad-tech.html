01 / The Problem

The Depreciation of Historic Targeting

Retail media networks are one of the fastest-growing ad channels. But they've been built on a crumbling foundation: third-party cookies and historical purchase data. The logic is intuitive — if a user bought running shoes, show them running gear ads.

Two structural shifts broke this model simultaneously. First, Chrome's cookie depreciation (following Firefox and Safari) stripped cross-site tracking. Second, purchase history data ages poorly: what a user bought 3 weeks ago predicts their current intent less reliably than what they've clicked on in the last 90 seconds.

Advertisers were watching CTR fall quarter-over-quarter. Not because the ad product was broken, but because the targeting signal was stale.

"Purchase history tells you what someone wanted. Session behavior tells you what they want right now. The gap between those two is where ad dollars go to die."

02 / The Hacky V1

Categorical Session Boosting

Our first attempt was naive but fast to ship: if a user clicked two items in the "Electronics" category within a session, we applied a flat 1.3x bid multiplier for electronics ads on their subsequent search page.

It outperformed the baseline by 6% CTR — enough to prove the directional hypothesis that in-session behavior was predictive. But it was full of problems:

  • It treated a 0.8-second accidental click identically to a 4-minute deep-read on a product spec sheet.
  • Category boosting was too coarse — a "Laptops" searcher and a "Phone Cases" searcher were both "Electronics," but had fundamentally different conversion propensities.
  • It had no model of intent intensity — whether someone was browsing casually or actively comparing final options.
03 / The Wall

Clickstream Noise at Scale

When we moved beyond clicks to track micro-interactions — scroll velocity, hover duration, facet toggle sequences, back-button frequency — the data volume exploded. We were ingesting millions of events per minute.

The signal-to-noise ratio was atrocious. Bot traffic, passive page-loads, and rapid browse-and-exit patterns produced behavioral signatures that looked identical to high-intent sessions in our raw feature vectors. Training a model on this noisy data produced propensity scores that were barely better than random at the item level.

We needed to distinguish the physics of a high-intent session from a casual browse — and do it in under 40ms to fit within the ad auction latency budget.

04 / The Real Solution

In-Session Behavioral DNN with Velocity Features

We rebuilt the targeting layer around a purpose-engineered feature set that captured the physics of user intent rather than categorical labels.

The key engineering decisions:

  • Velocity Features over Raw Clicks: We computed time-normalized interaction rates — e.g., scroll depth per second, dwell time relative to content length, image-vs-text attention ratio. These features captured how someone was engaging, not just whether they engaged.
  • Ephemeral Session Graph: Rather than a flat event log, we built a lightweight in-memory session graph that tracked interaction sequences. A user who viewed → filtered → zoomed → read specs had a fundamentally different graph topology than one who viewed → back → viewed → back.
  • DNN trained on conversion-as-truth: We labeled sessions by their eventual conversion within 72 hours and trained a shallow DNN (3 hidden layers) to predict this from the velocity feature set. The model never saw user IDs or historical data.
  • SKU-cluster targeting (not category): The model output was a propensity score per item cluster — groups of 8-12 semantically similar SKUs — enabling much more precise bid adjustments than category-level boosting.

05 / Results & Learnings

The most surprising finding: the in-session propensity engine outperformed cookie-based targeting by 22% CTR — even when cookie-based targeting had access to 30 days of purchase history. Recency dominates. What a user is doing right now is a stronger signal than what they bought last month.

The privacy constraint, which initially felt like a limitation, turned into a forcing function for better product design. By stripping away identity, we were forced to understand session behavior at a much deeper level than targeting tools historically required.
