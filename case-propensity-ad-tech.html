In-Session Propensity
for Ad Targeting

The ad industry is scrambling to replace cookies. But the strongest targeting signal was never purchase history — it's what a user is doing right now, in this session, in the last 90 seconds.

The Situation

A retail media network losing its targeting signal

Sponsored search ads within an e-commerce platform. Targeting historically relied on two signals: third-party cookies (cross-site behavior) and purchase history (audience segments built from what users bought in the last 30 days).

Both signals are simultaneously dying — cookies are being deprecated by browsers, and purchase history is stale by the time it's used (what someone bought 3 weeks ago is a weak predictor of what they want right now).

The Reframe

The targeting problem isn't "cookies are dying." It's that the industry is using the wrong signal class entirely.

The default assumption: "we need to find a cookie replacement — some other way to persistently identify users across sessions." This frames the problem as identity resolution.

The actual insight: recency dominates history. A user's behavior in the current session — what they're clicking, scrolling, dwelling on right now — is a stronger predictor of purchase intent than what they bought last month. The signal doesn't need to be persistent. It needs to be fresh.

Purchase history tells you what someone wanted. Session behavior tells you what they want right now. The gap between those two is where ad dollars go to die.

Why Option C: The category boost (Option B) proved the hypothesis — in-session behavior is predictive. But it couldn't distinguish casual browsing from active comparison shopping. The signal isn't what someone clicked — it's how they engaged. That requires measuring velocity, not just events.

The Bet

A behavioral DNN that reads session physics, not identity

The bet: you can build an accurate propensity model using only in-session behavioral velocity features — without ever knowing who the user is. The model reads the physics of engagement (how fast, how deep, what sequence) rather than the identity of the engager.

Four design choices that define this bet:

  • Velocity features, not raw events: Time-normalized interaction rates — scroll depth per second, dwell time relative to content length, image-vs-text attention ratio. Captures engagement intensity, not just occurrence.
  • Ephemeral session graph: In-memory graph tracking interaction sequences. A user who viewed → filtered → zoomed → read specs has a fundamentally different topology than view → back → view → back. Topology encodes intent stage.
  • Trained on conversion-as-truth: Label sessions by whether they convert within 72 hours. The DNN (3 hidden layers) learns to predict this from velocity features alone — no user IDs, no history.
  • SKU-cluster output, not category: Propensity score per cluster of 8-12 semantically similar items. Much more precise than category-level boosting — "wireless earbuds under ₹3K" vs just "Electronics."

Key Tradeoffs

The hard calls

  • SKU-cluster targeting vs category-level: Clusters are dramatically more precise but require ongoing maintenance (new products, seasonal shifts change cluster composition). Category-level is crude but stable. Chose clusters because the precision gain is large enough to justify the operational cost.
  • No warm-up period vs accuracy: The model produces a score from the first interaction, but early-session scores have low confidence. Could wait for 5+ interactions before activating — but that means no targeting on 40% of sessions (short sessions). Chose to score immediately with a confidence-weighted bid adjustment: low-confidence scores get smaller bid multipliers.
  • Privacy purity vs personalization: Could optionally link sessions for logged-in users to improve accuracy with cross-session features. Chose not to — keeping the system 100% identity-free simplifies compliance and makes it work identically for logged-out users (the majority).
Open Questions

What could go wrong

Bot traffic contamination: Sophisticated bots can mimic human-like interaction patterns. If bot sessions get labeled as "non-converting" (because bots don't buy), the model might learn to penalize sessions that look bot-like — which could include legitimate power users who browse quickly. Need bot-detection as a pre-filter, not a model feature.

Advertiser trust gap: Advertisers are accustomed to audience segment reports ("your ads were shown to 25-34 year old women interested in fitness"). In-session propensity is opaque — you can't explain WHO saw the ad, only that the session exhibited high-intent signals. This requires educating the advertiser base on a new measurement paradigm.

Cold-start for new product categories: The model trains on historical conversion data. A new product category with no conversion history has no training signal. Need a fallback to content-similarity scoring until sufficient conversion data accumulates (~2-4 weeks).
